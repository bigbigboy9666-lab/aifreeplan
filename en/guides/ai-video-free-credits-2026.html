How AI Video Generators Work: A Quick Primer

If you're new to AI video generation, understanding the basics will help you get better results from your free credits. Most AI video generators in 2026 use one of two approaches:

Text-to-video is the most common mode. You write a text prompt describing the scene you want — for example, "A golden retriever running through a sunlit meadow, cinematic camera tracking shot, 4K quality" — and the AI generates a short video clip matching that description. The quality of your prompt directly affects the quality of the output. Be specific about camera movements, lighting conditions, character actions, and visual style.

Image-to-video takes a still image as input and animates it. This is great for bringing photos, illustrations, or AI-generated images to life. Image-to-video typically produces more consistent results because the AI has a visual reference to work from. However, it often costs more credits than text-to-video on most platforms.

Some platforms also support video-to-video (transforming existing footage into a new style) and extend features (adding more seconds to an existing clip). These advanced features are usually limited to paid tiers but can be powerful for iterative creation.

Understanding Credit Costs

Not all generations cost the same number of credits. Here's what affects the cost:

  • Resolution: Higher resolution (1080p vs 720p) costs more credits
  • Duration: Longer clips cost proportionally more
  • Mode: Professional/HD mode costs more than standard mode
  • Motion complexity: Some platforms charge more for high-motion scenes
  • Model version: Newer, more capable models often cost more credits per generation

For example, on Kling AI, a standard 5-second clip might cost 6 credits, while a professional-mode 10-second clip could cost 20+ credits. This means your 66 daily credits could generate anywhere from 3 to 11 videos depending on your settings.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

New users often waste credits on avoidable mistakes. Here are the most common ones:

  • Vague prompts: "A dog running" will produce generic results. "A golden retriever puppy running joyfully through autumn leaves in a park, slow motion, warm golden hour lighting, cinematic 4K" gives the AI much more to work with.
  • Ignoring negative prompts: Many platforms support negative prompts (what you don't want). Use them to exclude common issues like distorted hands, blurry faces, or unwanted text.
  • Not iterating: Instead of generating 10 random clips, generate 2, identify what works, and refine your prompt before generating more. Iterative refinement produces better results with fewer credits.
  • Skipping the preview: Some platforms offer a low-cost preview or draft mode. Use it to check composition before spending full credits on HD output.
